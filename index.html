The question

Adaptive plasticity has long been known to help populations and species rapidly match their environment. But how does plasticity do this — and what does that mean for evolution?

Our lab focuses on shedding light on the genetic basis of phenotypic plasticity and on understanding the mechanisms behind it — whether that means how gene expression is affected, how cells change, or how developmental trajectories fork under different environmental circumstances. Ultimately, we want to find answers to some long-standing questions and theories of adaptive plasticity.

Flagship project

SLAP — Shedding Light on Adaptive Plasticity

Phenotypic plasticity — the ability of an organism to change its characteristics with changes in the environment — is an important mechanism by which populations can survive and flourish. Especially now, when human-induced environmental change is occurring all around the globe, it is important to understand how populations can produce different characters from the same genetic code. Our understanding of how this ability evolved is still limited.

The goal is to clarify:

  • What are the mechanisms that allow populations to change — that is, to show plasticity?
  • How does this ability evolve?

The Trinidadian guppy Poecilia reticulata offers an amazing, well-established system to address these questions. In a long-term selection experiment we expose guppies to different light environments and track the evolution of colour vision on a molecular and behavioural level. Cutting-edge methods reveal the genomic, transcriptomic and mechanistic basis of colour vision and its plasticity.

Dr. Nidal Karagic

Group Leader

What mainly interests me is how simple changes in the base pairs of a genetic code are translated into RNA, then proteins that make cells, tissues, and finally all the organismal diversity on our planet. Especially when this machinery can be tweaked by the environment to produce different outcomes within a species or population, I can't help but wonder how it works and what it means for evolutionary change. In my work, I focus more on the questions and less on a specific system or method.

I completed my PhD in Evolutionary Biology in 2022, working on the gene-regulatory and transcriptional basis of plasticity and adaptation in cichlid fishes. From 2022 to 2025 I was a postdoctoral researcher in the Integrative Evolutionary Biology Lab at the University of Helsinki, studying the genetic and mechanistic basis of colour patterns. I am now establishing my own group at the Institute of Biotechnology in Helsinki, working on the mechanistic basis of plasticity and on how plasticity, as a trait itself, evolves.

J. Exp. Zool. B

Heterochronic opsin expression due to early light deprivation results in drastically shifted visual sensitivity in a cichlid fish: possible role of thyroid hormone signalling

Colour vision under water is challenging: the light changes drastically with depth and turbidity, and these factors shift across a fish's lifetime. Cichlids adapt to match their needs at different developmental stages — the strongest effect appears when fish are reared in complete darkness, where colour vision races through every developmental stage within days and reaches an almost adult phenotype in very young embryos.

R. Soc. Open Sci.

Reverting ontogeny: rapid phenotypic plasticity of colour vision in cichlid fish

The light environment of fishes can change rapidly — for example, due to algal blooms or pollution — shifting from blue light in clear waters to greener or redder light. Within a few days Nicaraguan cichlids can match their colour vision to their environment, and they can reverse those changes when the light shifts a second time, at least as juveniles.

Mol. Biol. Evol.

A genomic cluster containing novel and conserved genes is associated with cichlid fish dental developmental convergence

One reason cichlids are among the most species-rich vertebrate groups may be their diverse, well-adapted pharyngeal jaws. Species feeding on soft items like algae develop countless small, pointed teeth; those feeding on hard items like snails develop few large, massive teeth. These extreme phenotypes evolved repeatedly and independently, and we show that gene birth gave rise to part of the underlying machinery.

04

Genome Biol. Evol.

Differential regulation of opsin gene expression in response to internal and external stimuli

Nicaraguan Midas cichlids rapidly adapt to new light environments. We tested whether developmental mechanisms were co-opted to allow plasticity to evolve so quickly, using a combination of light-environment and thyroid-hormone treatments. Plasticity and development (mimicked by changes in thyroid-hormone levels) turned out to be largely independent mechanisms, with little overlap.
